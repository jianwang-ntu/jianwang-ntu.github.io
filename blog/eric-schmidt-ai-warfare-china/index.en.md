# Eric Schmidt: AI Is Already Reshaping Warfare, and China Is Closer Than You Think

In a conversation moderated by journalist Tom Shanker, former Google CEO Eric Schmidt makes a sweeping argument: AI is not simply another technology wave — it is simultaneously rewriting the rules of warfare, software development, national-security infrastructure, and education. Schmidt's tone is urgent without being apocalyptic, and his sharpest claims are military and geopolitical.

## Ukraine as the Laboratory

Schmidt's most pointed military observation is that the battlefield has become "transparent." Anything that moves, he argues, can now be detected and destroyed by drones. That shift renders traditional large-platform military assets — tanks, artillery, armored vehicles — dangerously exposed, not merely less competitive.

He describes a future where soldiers survive by concealment in foxholes while automated systems — drones, swarms, AI-coordinated robotics — handle delivery, targeting, and engagement. The future of combat, in Schmidt's framing, belongs to robotic and autonomous systems operating in GPS-denied environments where human reaction time is no longer the binding constraint.

The implication he draws: militaries still investing mainly in traditional armor and manned platforms may be preparing for the wrong war.

## China's Six-Month Gap

On the AI race, Schmidt revises a view he had previously held publicly. He once estimated China was one to two years behind the United States in frontier AI capability. In this conversation, he places the gap at roughly six months — and attributes the compression to China's strategy of using less-advanced chips, leveraging open-source models, and diffusing its technology globally.

His concern is not only about raw capability. Schmidt notes that AI systems function as carriers of political and cultural values. Which side's models are most widely adopted will shape whose assumptions — about governance, information, and human rights — are embedded in global AI infrastructure.

## The End of Traditional Programming

Schmidt is direct about software development. He says coding as most practitioners have understood it is "over." The new work is goal specification, AI-agent supervision, and output validation. Programmers, in his framing, are becoming architects and supervisors rather than builders.

His prediction is bifurcated: the best technical people may become more valuable, while entry-level software jobs face immediate displacement pressure. He identifies junior developers and low-end customer-service workers as the roles already feeling the shift most sharply.

## Infrastructure as National-Security Issue

Schmidt argues that AI infrastructure — data centers, power supply, chips, and financing — now belongs in the category of strategic national assets. The scale of energy demand from large AI deployments means data centers may need to be co-located with dedicated power plants in remote areas, because the existing grid cannot readily support the load.

He frames the limiting factor not as energy per se, but as the capacity to finance infrastructure at the required scale. Governments, he says, need secure, classified AI data centers — not just for research but for operational military and intelligence use.

## Governance and Open-Source Risk

Schmidt is cautious about powerful open-weight models. He warns that globally diffused open-source AI could be modified by hostile actors for cyberattacks or other harmful applications. The governance problem, as he frames it, has moved beyond individual companies and become a geopolitical and security challenge requiring international agreements and multistakeholder oversight.

## Education

Schmidt argues that every university should require freshmen to learn how to work with AI tools, with the shift eventually reaching high schools. The emphasis, in his view, should move away from memorization and toward creativity, critical questioning, and the ability to supervise and validate AI outputs — the skills that matter most in a world where AI handles the routine generation of text, code, and analysis.
