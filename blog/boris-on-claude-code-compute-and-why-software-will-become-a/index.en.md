# Boris on Claude Code, Compute, and Why Software Will Become a Basic Literacy

![Boris on Claude Code, Compute, and Why Software Will Become a Basic Literacy](https://publicsg.s3.ap-southeast-1.amazonaws.com/github.io/images/blog/boris-on-claude-code-compute-and-why-software-will-become-a.png)

In a conversation at Anthropic's developer day, Boris — the creator of Claude Code — argues that AI coding agents are not just speeding engineers up, they are restructuring how companies work. The throughline of the talk: organizations that put Claude at the center of their business are seeing productivity climb by hundreds of percentage points, and the same shift is now spilling out from coding into every other kind of knowledge work.

## How Boris ended up building Claude Code

Boris describes a long career in startups — including being the first engineer at an early Y Combinator company — followed by seven years at Meta, where he was a tech lead for Instagram and owned code quality across the codebases. The common thread, he says, is wanting to ship good products, which to him means making engineers productive.

He was living in Japan when he decided to join Anthropic, framing the move in safety terms: "this thing has to be safe and it has to be done the right way." He joined what was then Anthropic Labs, a team that has since been re-formed under Mike Krieger, one of the Instagram co-founders. According to Boris, that team produced four things — Claude Code, MCP, Skills, and the desktop app — and the focus on enterprise, safety, and coding traces back through Anthropic's history.

## What got announced at developer day

Boris is upfront that the day was light on headline news: no new model, no big product launch. The announcements he highlights:

- **Managed agents API** — a way for developers to build agents on Anthropic's platform with the tools wired in, so building agents is easier and smoother.
- **Compute** — Colossus 1 will be dedicated to serving Anthropic's customers, a partnership with SpaceX that brings substantially more compute online.

On the compute question, Boris says growth has "outpac[ed] every estimate," and that internally the balance between research and product compute is treated as a long-term planning problem rather than a fight over scarce resources. The goal, he says, is to neither over-buy nor under-buy.

## Coding has already changed — and Boris thinks every desk job is next

About six months ago, Boris says, the way he writes code flipped: he no longer types it, he prompts Claude to write it. At any given moment he has "a few agents or sometimes thousands of agents" doing his work. He places the inflection point at Opus 4.5 last November, accelerating through 4.6 and 4.7.

He generalizes from there. Engineers, he argues, are always the first adopters; what they're doing now is what every computer-based job will look like over the next few years. The first non-coding product in that direction is **Co-work**, which Boris says was born from watching customers use Claude Code for things it wasn't built for — data analytics, project management, even a Twitter user growing tomato plants by monitoring nutrients on a webcam. Co-work, he claims, is taking off faster than Claude Code did at the same stage.

## Why most companies haven't seen the gains yet

Boris reaches for a Harvard Business School case study from the early 1990s asking why computers hadn't produced the productivity boom they were supposed to. The answer in that piece, as he recounts it: you only get the gains if you restructure the entire business around the computer — not park a PC next to the filing cabinets.

He says Anthropic's most sophisticated customers are doing exactly that with Claude. Where his old developer-productivity work at Meta moved numbers a few percentage points, the customers restructuring around Claude are seeing hundreds of percentage points of improvement, and the rate of improvement is itself accelerating. He cites Shopify and NASA — the latter using Claude Code to plot a Mars rover course — as examples, and predicts knowledge-work adoption catches up over the next 6 to 12 months.

## What it means for jobs, moats, and software companies

Pressed on employment, Boris stays in the short term: the immediate effect he sees is people freed from "toilome" work. He gives a personal example — Co-work booked all his plane tickets for the developer event while he did something else. He defers longer-term predictions to economists.

On corporate moats, his view is mixed. **Switching costs are eroding** — Claude can rewrite software, making it easier to migrate from one tool to another. But most other moats, he argues, are "just as powerful as they were before"; the whole game is just running faster.

He sidesteps a question about whether Anthropic wants to be the front door that owns the customer relationship. His framing: Anthropic wants to own some relationships directly, but also wants to be fair, so the same tools it uses internally — the Claude Agent SDK, the Anthropic API — are available to outside developers, with thousands of startups already building on that stack.

## Software literacy as the printing-press moment

Asked whether entry-level software jobs are going away, Boris reframes the question. His prediction: writing software becomes a basic skill, the way reading and writing did after the 1400s. Most people will be able to talk to Claude and get code written. Professional engineers will still matter — the way professional writers still matter in a literate society — but the floor of what an ordinary person can build rises dramatically. When the interviewer calls it a "printing press moment," Boris agrees.

He hedges on some of the second-order questions. Asked what app stores look like in five to ten years if anyone can spin up an app on demand, he punts: "I'd love to talk to the people that work on app stores and get their take on that."

## Security: the threat model just changed

Boris calls security one of Anthropic's top priorities and says the models have, in the last three months, become "exceptional at finding vulnerabilities in every kind of software that exists." That, he says, is a phase change — the threat dynamics are new on both sides. Anthropic's framing is to make sure defenders have the best model first, so they can find and patch vulnerabilities before attackers exploit them. He confirms there are no plans to broadly roll out Mythos, the security tool the interviewer asks about.

On trusting an agent with real work, his only practical advice is to keep retrying as new models ship, because each release is "completely different."

## Talent, competition, and advice for new graduates

Boris pushes back on the idea that better tools lower the bar for hiring. Good people — engineers, PMs, designers — still matter a lot, he says. He frames Anthropic's competitive position around focus: developers, coding, and enterprise have always been the priority, and that isn't changing.

For students entering this market, he gives two pieces of advice:

1. **Learn the tools.** Don't be afraid of agents. Try them, see what works.
2. **Start a startup, if you're inclined.** He calls this "the golden age" for founders and predicts there will be "10 times more startups… 100 times more" within a decade.

The takeaway Boris leaves the room with is the literacy analogy: the people and companies that treat AI as central, not peripheral, are the ones already pulling away — and the rest of the economy has roughly a year to catch up.
