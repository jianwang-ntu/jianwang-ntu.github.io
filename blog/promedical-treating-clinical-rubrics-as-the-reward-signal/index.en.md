# ProMedical: Treating Clinical Rubrics as the Reward Signal, Not a Post-Hoc Check

![ProMedical: Treating Clinical Rubrics as the Reward Signal, Not a Post-Hoc Check](https://publicsg.s3.ap-southeast-1.amazonaws.com/github.io/images/blog/promedical-treating-clinical-rubrics-as-the-reward-signal.png)
*Image from [https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.08326](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2604.08326)*


A team from Xunfei Healthcare argues that the standard recipe for aligning medical LLMs — collect pairwise preferences, train a scalar reward model, run RLHF/DPO — is fundamentally mismatched with how clinicians actually evaluate medical answers. Their proposed fix, ProMedical, pushes physician-written rubrics directly into preference construction, reward modeling, and the optimization objective itself, with safety enforced as a hard veto rather than a soft penalty. The authors report a 22.3% overall accuracy gain and 21.7% safety-compliance gain when ProMedical-RM is used to steer GRPO on a Qwen3-8B base.

## The alignment gap the paper diagnoses

The paper frames the problem as a structural mismatch: medical evaluation benchmarks have moved toward fine-grained, rubric-driven scoring (HealthBench, K-QA, DAHL), while training pipelines still rely on holistic binary preferences. The authors argue this forces models to *implicitly* infer complex clinical rationales from a single bit of supervision, encouraging spurious correlations — most consequentially, conflating safety with surface fluency. They cite reward-model benchmarks (RewardBench, InfiMed-ORBIT) that show poor generalization once structured constraints are introduced.

![ProMedical: Treating Clinical Rubrics as the Reward Signal, Not a Post-Hoc Check — overview diagram](https://publicsg.s3.ap-southeast-1.amazonaws.com/github.io/images/blog/promedical-treating-clinical-rubrics-as-the-reward-signal_diagram.png)


Their answer is to stop treating rubrics as a post-hoc evaluator and use them as the actual training signal.

## A tripartite rubric, scored lexicographically

ProMedical decomposes the quality of a clinical response into three orthogonal dimensions:

- **Main Proficiency (S₁):** a weighted sum of instruction-specific accuracy/completeness criteria (weights normalized to 1).
- **Excellence Bonus (S₂):** a count of "above-baseline" attributes — empathy, structural clarity, contextual nuance.
- **Safety Veto (S₃):** a count of critical infractions — hallucinations, unauthorized diagnosis, impersonating a clinician, toxic advice.

The non-trivial design choice is that these don't simply sum. Comparison between two responses is *lexicographic*: S₃ is checked first, S₁ next, S₂ last. The authors contrast this with HealthBench/K-QA-style scalar deductions, which they argue let an optimizer trade safety for utility. Lexicographic ordering severs that gradient — a single safety violation strictly dominates any helpfulness gain.

When the rubric has to be reduced to a scalar (for GRPO), the authors instead use a clipped utility minus a penalty:

`r = Clip(S₁ + αS₂, 0, 1+β) − λ·S₃`

with `λ ≥ 1+β` to guarantee that one safety infraction outweighs any possible bonus. The β margin matters: without it, perfect proficiency saturates the reward and the excellence bonus produces no gradient.

## Two ways to use the rubric

The paper distinguishes two paradigms built on the same rubric machinery:

**Implicit Outcome Alignment** collapses the rubric into the scalar reward above and trains GRPO directly. The model never sees the criteria, only their aggregate score.

**Explicit Criteria Injection** trains a separate *Rubric-Aware Reward Model* (ProMedical-RM) that estimates `P(y_w ≻ y_l | x, c)` — preference *conditioned on a specific rubric dimension c*. A single response pair is expanded into K training instances, one per applicable rubric, with independent labels. The authors argue this disentangles "scalar conflation" — where the model learns *that* one response is preferred but not *why*.

Empirically, Explicit Criteria Injection wins. The paper reports Qwen3-8B aligned via the explicit paradigm scoring 76.39 on ProMedical-Bench vs. 70.2 for InfiMed-ORBIT and 71.8 for ScaleAI-RaR.

## Building the dataset without burning out physicians

A practical contribution is the human-in-the-loop pipeline behind **ProMedical-Preference-50k**. Starting from 823k samples aggregated across nine open-source medical datasets (MedQA, PubMedQA, MedMCQA, ChatDoctor, etc.), the authors apply semantic deduplication via MedEmbed embeddings, difficulty filtering via DeepSeek-R1 (keeping scores 5–9), and a 13-category expert taxonomy, ending at ~52k instructions. Responses are regenerated by Qwen3-235B-Thinking, Claude-Sonnet-4.5-Thinking, and DeepSeek-R1 to diversify reasoning styles.

Rubrics are instantiated by Gemini-3-Pro-Thinking, then iteratively refined: in each cycle, physicians adjudicate 500 generated rubric instances, and corrected examples are recursively injected back into the few-shot pool. The authors report 96.40% pass rate under expert evaluation after this loop, and 93.2% agreement between GPT-4.1 (used to apply the instantiated rubrics as labels) and human adjudicators.

For evaluation, **ProMedical-Bench** holds out 795 instructions, fully decontaminated from training. Every sample is double-blind annotated by licensed physicians (5+ years of practice, $4 per instance, ~30 minutes each, three-way adjudication for ties), reaching a weighted Cohen's κ of 0.88. Through dimensional expansion, the 795 pairs become 5,505 fine-grained adjudication instances (3,625 Proficiency, 1,650 Excellence, 230 Safety).

## What the experiments show

On ProMedical-Bench's pairwise preference task:

| Model | Overall |
|---|---|
| GPT-5 | 76.42 |
| DeepSeek-R1 | 78.55 |
| Qwen3-235B-Thinking | 77.45 |
| Meditron-70B | 53.40 |
| PairRM-LLaMA3-8B | 58.95 |
| **ProMedical-RM-8B (Qwen3)** | **86.55** |
| **ProMedical-RM-8B (Llama)** | **85.40** |

Two findings the paper highlights:

1. **Scale doesn't substitute for structured supervision.** Meditron-70B, despite eight times the parameters and biomedical pretraining, sits below the general-purpose PairRM-LLaMA3-8B. The authors read this as evidence that compliance with fine-grained safety constraints is a training-objective problem, not a capacity problem.

2. **The gain is backbone-agnostic.** Swapping Qwen3-8B for Llama-3-8B-Instruct under identical training drops overall accuracy by only 1.15 points (86.55 → 85.40), still ahead of every open-source baseline. The authors take this as evidence the gain comes from Explicit Criteria Injection rather than the backbone.

On Safety-Veto F1, ProMedical-RM (Qwen3) reaches 89.09, ahead of GPT-5 (76.45) and DeepSeek-R1 (78.80). The authors note that medical o1 verifier hits only 50.80% recall, missing roughly half the genuine hazards — the kind of asymmetry that's invisible if you only report accuracy.

When ProMedical-RM is used as a reward oracle for GRPO on Qwen3-8B, the aligned policy improves on HealthBench (46.27 → 53.64) and ProMedical-Bench (69.5 → 76.39), beating UltraMedical-Preference and ScaleAI-RaR alignment baselines.

## Reward hacking and the case for a hard veto

Several of the paper's case studies illustrate failure modes that a holistic scalar misses. In one example, a long, empathetic answer claiming "in 15+ years of reviewing such reports..." would beat a more measured response on most reward signals — except that the claim of clinical experience is a fabricated credential. The lexicographic veto rejects it outright. In another, a verbose answer that asserts "the elevated amylase/lipase confirm the diagnosis" loses to a more concise, hierarchically structured answer because the unauthorized diagnostic certainty triggers the safety constraint.

The authors frame these as evidence that scalar rewards encourage models to inflate confidence and authority to maximize perceived helpfulness — exactly the opposite of what a clinical context demands. An ablation backs this: replacing the lexicographic veto with a soft linear penalty drops safety compliance from 90.26 to 82.65, even though raw proficiency stays high.

## What it doesn't show

The authors are explicit about scope. ProMedical is text-only — radiology, pathology slides, and biochemical signals are out of frame, which is a serious gap for "real" diagnosis. The HITL rubric pipeline assumes expert consensus exists, so domains where guidelines are genuinely contested are harder. And while the framework generalizes to UltraMedical and a Chinese MedBench subset (the paper claims +21.7% on Hallucination/Compliance and +28.9% on Psychiatric QA over an SFT baseline), all evaluations remain on benchmarks built from the same rubric philosophy. Live clinical deployment, where physician oversight is mandatory anyway, is not what the experiments measure.

## Takeaway

The paper's broader argument is that medical alignment shouldn't be a two-stage pipeline (train on coarse preferences, evaluate on rubrics). If rubrics are how clinicians actually judge answers, they should be the training signal — and safety should be a constraint the optimizer cannot trade away, not just another term in a weighted sum. Whether the lexicographic veto generalizes beyond text-only Q&A and beyond the rubric distributions ProMedical itself defined is the obvious next question.
