# Builders Win, Information Movers Don't: Nikhyl Singhal on the Next Two Years of Product Management

![Builders Win, Information Movers Don't: Nikhyl Singhal on the Next Two Years of Product Management](/images/blog/builders-win-information-movers-dont-nikhyl-singhal-on-the.png)

In a return appearance on Lenny's Podcast, Nikhyl Singhal — former exec at Meta and Google, ex-CPO at Credit Karma, and convener of the Skip community of heads of product — argues that the product industry is in a renaissance for builders and a reckoning for everyone else. His core claim: in the next 12 to 24 months, companies will shed staff at a scale that swamps the AI-first rehiring that follows, and the people who survive will be those who cross a specific psychological threshold he calls reinvention.

## A renaissance for builders, a dead end for information movers

Singhal opens by contrasting today with the late-ZIRP era he and Lenny discussed two or three years ago. Back then, product managers were paid well but unhappy: their days consisted of "moving information from one to another," framing a team's work so a boss could reframe it for the boss's boss. The function had drifted into responsibility without authority — what Singhal calls the greatest form of workplace stress.

What changed, he argues, is that strong builders can now act directly on their instincts. They don't need to convince a designer or wait for a backlog grooming. They prototype, ship, and test. Compensation for top builders is at an all-time high. More of them are taking founder or non-product C-suite roles than ever — Singhal notes that his community of roughly 125 heads of product had one founder during its first two to three years and now has fourteen in the last twelve months alone. One member, he says, recently interviewed for a CHRO role because the company wanted someone with a product builder's judgment, empowerment, and obsolescence instincts.

The flip side is bleak. Singhal estimates that roughly half of the people who became PMs in the last decade did so because they were good at moving information and team-building, not because they loved building software. Those people, he says, are "in trouble." The "information mover," in his framing, is becoming a dinosaur.

## The shedding-and-rehiring prediction

Singhal expects the next 12 to 24 months to bring "massive shedding of staffs and then massive rehiring," but not a wash. He gives an illustrative ratio: a company might shed 30,000 and hire 8,000, with the 8,000 all being AI-first. The shedding, he argues, comes from two pressures stacking. Companies that doubled headcount over the last five years are asking whether they got twice as much output (he doubts it — at his old employer Google, he and colleagues used to joke that only a fraction of the workforce was truly load-bearing). At the same time, the new skill set required is fundamentally different from what most of the existing org was hired for.

Lenny points out a counter-signal from his recent jobs report: there are more open PM roles globally at tech companies than at any point in three-plus years, the highest since COVID. Singhal's read is that both things are true once you split "product manager" into two archetypes. The builders are in extraordinary demand. The information movers are watching their jobs disappear.

## What companies look like now

At a recent meetup of about 125 heads of product, Singhal ran a show-and-tell of the internal tools attendees were building. What struck him, he says, was that the vocabulary — agents, chief-of-staff apps, automated standups and product reviews, judgment as the bottleneck — was unrecognizable from twelve months earlier.

He sketches the trajectory:

- Product leaders increasingly get paid for **judgment** — deciding whether a change is good or bad, whether to ship one version or another, whether the system as a whole still hangs together. With ten to a hundred times more product changes flowing through, that judgment becomes paramount.
- Most companies will obsolete the mechanical parts of product work within two years.
- Bad software gets fixed, because someone just sits down and tells Claude to fix it. Singhal admits this is "maybe more of a wish than a prediction," but argues the unloved apps controlling shades, garage doors, and airline accounts — much of it written years ago by engineers who don't care, sometimes built on COBOL by engineers who have literally died — are now economically feasible to repair.

On the question of whether PMs should be coding themselves, Singhal pushes back on a take Lenny brings from Aamol, Anthropic's head of growth, that PMs are higher-leverage when they don't ship code. Singhal's distinction: a PM trying to be a 51st engineer on a team of 50 is a cheap knockoff. A PM building tools that change how the team makes decisions — automating status reports, product reviews, standups — is reshaping the product operating system. That's where the joy and the leverage compound.

## Why this is so hard for the people who were good at the old game

The most striking psychological observation in the talk: the people who mastered the previous version of product management are the ones who struggle most to reinvent. Singhal calls this the "shadow superpower." If the old way worked for you, you have no incentive to change, and your employer's praise reinforces the trap. The weakest practitioners, paradoxically, find it easiest to reinvent because they had less to lose.

He layers this with the demands of mid-career life. Anyone in their thirties is in their "power years" — peak energy, peak competence — but simultaneously dealing with partners, kids, aging parents, the body's first warnings. Singhal's blunt formulation: "Your goal in your power years is to equally disappoint everyone." Adding "stay current on cloud code" to that triage is genuinely hard.

And the target keeps moving. Take a week off to catch up and three months later that week is antiquated. This isn't a one-time transition; it's a continuous one — at least for the next couple of years.

## The reinvention threshold, and the moment of joy

Singhal's central piece of advice is that every product person has to find the will to cross a threshold of embracing reinvention. He claims the crossing typically happens through a specific moment: a builder catches the bug. They stay up late building something silly — a chief-of-staff app for their inbox, an agent to match members in a community, lights in the house — and the satisfaction is unlike anything they get from a normal PM day.

He frames product management as historically low-joy work. As a PM, he says, he used to change light bulbs as a break because at least there was a broken thing that got fixed. Now builders can participate in that satisfaction inside their actual job. His prediction: in a world where most PM time becomes building rather than information shuttling, joy displaces work, and joy is the strongest antidote to burnout.

For leaders, he adds, the implication is to find and amplify moments of joy in their staff. It's contagious.

## Brands matter less, modernity matters more

Singhal flags a hiring shift that contradicts earlier career advice — including advice he gave on his previous appearance on the show. Resume logos are losing weight. Interviewers increasingly ask scenario questions: which tools do you use, how do you think, what's your judgment. A six-year stint at a prestigious company can actually hurt if that company operated in a way that's now alien. Talking about how you ground out an algorithm improvement at Meta, he says, "falls very flat" in a conversation with someone living in the future.

The skills hierarchy, in his telling, has nearly flipped upside down. Careers used to mature from building toward leverage, scale, and organizational empowerment — stop working on the car, work on the factory. Now the factory itself is being rebuilt, and the question is what you can build and what your opinion is. That's jarring for people who climbed the old ladder.

## Specific advice for the next two years

When pressed on what listeners should actually do, Singhal lists:

- **Find the moment of joy.** Build one thing that solves a personal annoyance. Catch the bug.
- **Obsolete yourself.** Borrow the engineer's mindset he learned in his first job: a great engineer obsoletes themselves from everything they do. AI now lets non-engineers do this too.
- **Increase pace.** The next two years require the energy of a first year on a new job or the first year of a new relationship. Build the reserve, even if it means disappointing others more than usual.
- **Drop the ego.** Don't insist on the same title you held last. If everything is changing, the title from the old world doesn't price the new one.
- **Stay long-term focused.** Optimize for the "skip" job — the move after next — and accept that the next move is about getting on the boat to the new world, not maximizing immediate level.

## A worry he names out loud

Singhal flags one concern he says the industry isn't talking about: diversity is taking a step back. When companies hire fewer people and demand they spend nights and weekends feeding the LLM, hiring biases toward people who look and act like the people already in the room. Age, gender, geographic, and ethnic diversity are all, he believes, regressing. Women in their power years often can't give up nights and weekends for cloud code. He doesn't claim anyone is doing this intentionally — it's an emergent property of pace.

## Where the role goes from here

Singhal expects three things to happen at once over the next several years, which is part of why people feel disoriented. Product leaders will "blow like dandelion seeds" into adjacent functions and industries as the change agents who can read both technology and organizations. People from design, data science, and engineering will move into product because they have the judgment and opinions the role now requires. And a population of current PMs will struggle to find a way in at all.

He pushes back gently on the framing that this acceleration lasts forever. The current chaos, he argues, is comparable to the birth of internet-era product management at companies like Google — a few years of jarring redefinition before the role stabilizes into something teachable and consistent again. The merry-go-round won't keep spinning faster forever. But right now, it is spinning.

## The line that sums it up

Singhal closes on Einstein's "genius is one percent inspiration, ninety-nine percent perspiration" — his high school yearbook quote — reread through the lens of AI. The AI, he says, will do the perspiration. Unlimited. The work that remains, and the work that distinguishes people, is the inspiration. The people who find that inspiration cross the threshold. The ones who don't, in his framing, are watching the industry leave the station without them.

Asked at the end what he sees in his community now versus before, Singhal offers the line that probably captures the talk best: "smiling exhaustion" instead of just exhaustion. He'll take it.
